Regular Expressions

Imagine you're a detective looking for a suspect. You don't have a photo, but you have a description:

"A tall person wearing a red hat."

Most computer searches are exact: you search "John" and it finds "John".

Regular Expressions (Regex) allow you to search by description. Instead of searching for a specific word, you search for a pattern. It's a "composite sketch" for data.

The Syntax Decoded

Master the building blocks of pattern matching.

The Exact Match
/abc/

Matches the exact characters "abc". This is the most basic search, just like Ctrl+F.

The Category
\d or \w

Shorthand for types of characters.
\d = Any Digit (0-9)
\w = Any Word Char

The Repeater
{3} or +

How many times the previous rule applies.
{3} = Exactly 3 times.
+ = 1 or more times.

The Wildcard
.

The dot matches any single character (except newlines). It's the ultimate chameleon.

The Mathematics

Formal definitions behind regular expressions.

Formal Definition

A regular expression \( R \) over an alphabet \( \Sigma \) is defined recursively as:

\[ R ::= \emptyset \mid \epsilon \mid a \mid R_1 \cdot R_2 \mid R_1 | R_2 \mid R^* \]

Where \( a \in \Sigma \), \( \emptyset \) is the empty set, and \( \epsilon \) is the empty string.

Language of a Regex

The language \( L(R) \) denoted by a regular expression \( R \) is defined as:

\[ \begin{aligned} L(\emptyset) &= \emptyset \\ L(\epsilon) &= \{\epsilon\} \\ L(a) &= \{a\} \\ L(R_1 \cdot R_2) &= L(R_1) \cdot L(R_2) \\ L(R_1 | R_2) &= L(R_1) \cup L(R_2) \\ L(R^*) &= L(R)^* \end{aligned} \]

Kleene's Theorem

A language is regular if and only if it can be described by a regular expression. Equivalently, a language is regular iff it is accepted by a finite automaton (DFA or NFA).

\[ \text{Regular Languages} = \text{DFA} = \text{NFA} = \text{Regex} \]

This is just the beginning. Regular expressions are a superpower for data processing, text validation, and pattern matching across all programming languages.
